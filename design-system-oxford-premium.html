Building a Design System

7 min read

TL;DR

I led the creation of a design system for Oxford Premium as part of a wider rebranding and platform redesign initiative.

The project aimed to simplify a complex educational ecosystem, improve consistency, support accessibility requirements and bring the experience closer to Oxford’s UK platform, HUB.

I worked as the sole UX/UI Designer, with full ownership across research, visual direction, interaction design, design system foundations, documentation and implementation support.

1. Overview

Role: Sole UX/UI Designer

Project duration: 11 months

Design and strategy: 7 months

Implementation and support: 4 months

Scope: Platform-wide redesign, information architecture and design system foundations.

Tools: Figma, FigJam, Hotjar, Maze and Jira.

The project covered the redesign of Oxford Premium’s experience, the creation of a new visual direction and the foundations of a scalable design system.

It was not a UI kit or a small component library. It was a full redesign initiative connected to product strategy, platform structure, visual alignment and implementation.

2. Context

Oxford Premium is a complex educational platform that brings together digital books, teaching resources, assessments, professional development content and other learning tools.

Over time, the platform had grown significantly. Different areas had evolved at different moments, which created inconsistencies in navigation, resource organisation, visual language and interaction patterns.

At the same time, the platform was going through a rebranding process and there was a long-term objective of bringing the experience closer to HUB, Oxford’s UK platform.

This created an opportunity to rethink the platform from a systems perspective, not only by redesigning screens, but by creating a more coherent foundation for the whole experience.

3. My Role

I was the only designer working on the initiative and had full ownership from strategy to implementation support.

My work included:

  • Research and analysis
  • Information architecture
  • UX strategy
  • Interaction design
  • Visual direction
  • Design system foundations
  • Documentation
  • Implementation support

I worked directly with product, development, editorial, leadership and UK teams.

A significant part of the work involved presenting proposals, discussing trade-offs and validating visual and structural decisions as the project evolved.

4. Understanding the Ecosystem

Before designing, I needed to understand how the platform actually worked.

I mapped products, services, resource structures, navigation patterns, existing journeys and platform dependencies.

The maps quickly became quite large.

More importantly, they revealed that the platform had gradually been organised around internal structures and historical decisions rather than around how teachers actually used it.

The problem was not a lack of content. The problem was complexity, discoverability and consistency.

5. Design Process

This was not a linear project.

Several activities happened in parallel and continuously informed each other:

  • Comparative analysis with HUB
  • Information architecture mapping
  • User flows
  • Interaction redesign
  • Wireframing
  • High-fidelity designs
  • Prototyping
  • Documentation
  • Design system foundations
  • Validation with stakeholders
  • Progressive implementation with development

The project constantly moved between understanding the current experience, proposing solutions and refining decisions as new information appeared.

The work also connected directly with the wider redesign of Oxford Premium: navigation, resource browsing, search behaviour, filters, responsive layouts and reusable components all had to evolve together.

6. Visual Alignment with HUB

One of the strategic objectives was to bring Oxford Premium closer to HUB while respecting the needs and complexity of the local product.

This was not about copying one platform into another.

Oxford Premium had more services, more resource types and different content needs. Some HUB patterns could be used as reference, but many decisions needed to be adapted.

The work involved understanding HUB’s visual and interaction principles, identifying opportunities for alignment, exploring new visual directions, adapting interaction patterns where appropriate and balancing consistency with local product needs.

Throughout the project, I regularly presented and validated proposals with leadership and the UK team.

7. Redesigning Interaction Flows

The work went far beyond reorganising information.

I redesigned the interaction model itself:

  • Entry points
  • Navigation flows
  • Resource browsing
  • Search behaviours
  • Filtering patterns
  • Resource hierarchy

A lot of the work focused on reducing unnecessary decisions and making navigation more predictable.

Rather than asking teachers to understand how the platform was organised internally, the experience was reorganised around what they were trying to achieve.

8. Design System Foundations

As the redesign progressed, it became clear that consistency could not be solved screen by screen.

I created the foundations for a design system covering:

  • Typography
  • Colour system
  • Components
  • Spacing rules
  • Responsive behaviours
  • Accessibility requirements
  • Documentation

The goal was to create a shared foundation that could support future work and reduce implementation complexity.

Instead of treating each screen as an isolated solution, the system created a shared language for future product work.

9. Progressive Implementation

The implementation phase lasted four months and happened progressively.

I worked closely with developers to clarify behaviours, produce documentation, review implementations, support handoff and iterate on solutions.

Because the project touched almost every area of the platform, maintaining alignment between design and development became just as important as the design work itself.

10. Outcome

The project delivered:

  • A new information architecture
  • Simplified navigation patterns
  • Redesigned interaction flows
  • Improved discoverability
  • Greater alignment with HUB
  • Foundations for a scalable design system
  • A complete redesign of the platform experience

More importantly, it helped reduce complexity in a platform that had grown faster than its structure.

11. Reflection

One thing this project reinforced for me is that the biggest UX problems are rarely caused by missing features.

More often, they appear when products grow faster than their structure.

This project was not really about redesigning screens. It was about reducing complexity and helping people spend less time understanding the platform and more time using it.
